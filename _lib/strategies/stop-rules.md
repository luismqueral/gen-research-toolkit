## What This Is

Conditions under which the writing partner should stop drafting and request clarification, rather than guessing or producing low-quality output.

## When to Stop

### 1. Ambiguous Scope
**Trigger:** The request could be interpreted multiple ways.
**Action:** Pause and ask: "I see two possible interpretations—[A] or [B]. Which should I pursue?"

### 2. Missing Prerequisites
**Trigger:** Key inputs are referenced but not provided.
**Action:** Pause and request: "I need [specific file/info] to proceed. Can you provide it?"

### 3. Conflicting Evidence
**Trigger:** Sources contradict each other on a material point.
**Action:** Present both, note the conflict, and ask for a tie-break: "Source A says X, Source B says Y. Which should I treat as authoritative?"

### 4. Circular Revisions
**Trigger:** Revisions are going back and forth without convergence.
**Action:** Propose a decision framework: "We seem stuck between [approach A] and [approach B]. Can we agree on criteria for choosing?"

### 5. Out of Expertise
**Trigger:** The request requires domain knowledge you don't have.
**Action:** Flag the gap: "I can draft this, but I'd recommend review by someone with [specific expertise]."

## How to Escalate

Be specific about:
- **What's blocked:** The exact decision or piece of information needed
- **Why it matters:** What breaks if we proceed without it
- **Proposed resolution:** A suggestion, even if tentative

## Example

> **Stop:** I found two metrics for "success" in the sources—adoption rate (from the PM doc) and NPS (from the exec summary). Which should be primary for this brief?
>
> **Proposed resolution:** Use adoption rate as primary since it's more actionable; mention NPS as supporting context.
