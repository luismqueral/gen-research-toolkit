## What This Is

When quoting transcripts or colloquial conversations, insert short, square-bracketed context inside the quote to disambiguate references, pronouns, or implied subjects—without changing the speaker's meaning or tone.

## Why It Matters

- Transcripts are messy; pronouns and shorthand often hide the referent
- Readers should grasp intent without re-reading full transcripts
- Careful annotations preserve fidelity while improving clarity

## How to Apply

- Add concise square-bracket clarifications inline: `[context here]`
- Keep each bracket to 2–6 words; stack multiple brackets if needed
- Clarify who/what/when, not "why they felt that way"
- Do not fix grammar or polish wording—only clarify referents
- Do not change sentiment, exaggerate, or soften impact
- Prefer nouns over restating claims
- Prioritize high-value context (entity, scope, system)
- Include a source pointer (file path + line range) nearby when possible

## Examples

**Original:** "I just wanna see if it like aligns."
**Annotated:** "I just wanna see if [our shared model of the work ahead] aligns."

**Original:** "I cannot tell you today what access you have to all of these systems without me going..."
**Annotated:** "I cannot tell you today what access you have [across our core systems] without me going [system-by-system]."

**Original:** "multiple data sources, inconsistent governance across the place..."
**Annotated:** "multiple data sources, inconsistent governance [across teams/tools]..."

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Bracketed text is minimal, factual, and disambiguating
- [ ] No tone shift; no edits outside brackets
- [ ] Source reference provided when used in briefs/syntheses
